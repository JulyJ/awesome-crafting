    Complete Guide to Paper Types for Crafting

    Understanding Paper for Crafting Success

    Choosing the right paper transforms craft projects from amateur to professional. Paper weight, finish, fiber content, and purpose all determine whether your creation holds up, looks polished, or falls apart. This guide demystifies paper terminology and helps you select the perfect paper for every project.

    Paper Weight Explained

    Weight (lb) Weight (gsm) Thickness Feel Common Uses Can It Fold?
    20-24 lb 75-90 gsm Thin, like copy paper Origami, tissue overlays, pattern tracing Excellent
    28-32 lb 105-120 gsm Light, like magazine Lightweight cards, liners, paper piecing Very good
    65-80 lb 176-218 gsm Medium, like cardstock Card bases, scrapbook pages, die-cutting Good with scoring
    80-100 lb 218-271 gsm Heavy cardstock Sturdy cards, boxes, structure Requires scoring
    110-130 lb 298-350 gsm Very heavy Premium cards, covers, stands alone Difficult, deep scoring needed

    Note: Paper weight systems differ. Text weight (20-80 lb) and cover weight (65-130 lb) measure different paper sizes, so 80 lb text ≠ 80 lb cover. GSM (grams per square meter) is universal and more reliable.

    Acid-Free and Archival Quality

    Paper Quality pH Level Longevity Best For Cost Difference
    Acidic Paper Below pH 7 10-50 years, yellows, brittle Temporary projects, practice Standard
    Acid-Free pH 7 or above 100+ years, stable Scrapbooks, photos, keepsakes +10-30%
    Archival Quality pH 7+, lignin-free, buffered 200+ years, museum-quality Important documents, fine art, preservation +30-80%

    When It Matters: Always use acid-free paper for scrapbooks, photo storage, or anything meant to last. Acid migrates and can damage adjacent photos and papers over time.

    Grain Direction

    Grain How to Test Why It Matters Best Practice
    Long Grain Paper folds smoother parallel to long edge Cleaner folds, less cracking Fold with the grain
    Short Grain Paper folds smoother parallel to short edge Important for bookbinding, cards Align grain with spine/fold
    Testing Method Gently bend (don’t crease) both directions—easier direction = with grain Affects folding, tearing, curling Always test before cutting large quantities

    Paper Storage Best Practices

    Storage Method Purpose Cost Best For
    Flat File/Drawer Keep flat, organized, protected \($-\)$$ Large collections, professionals
    Vertical File Box Store vertically, easy access $$ 12x12 scrapbook paper, moderate collections
    Sheet Protectors in Binder Protect individual sheets, visible $-$$ Specialty papers, small amounts, organization
    Paper Rack/Sorter Sort by color, quick access \(-\)$ Frequently used papers, active projects
    Original Packaging Keep paper protected until use Free Unused packs, space-saving
    Climate-Controlled Prevent warping, fading, moisture Varies All paper, especially archival

    Key Tips: Store paper flat or vertically (never bent), away from direct sunlight, in low humidity (30-50%), and away from acidic materials.

    Common Paper Problems and Solutions

    Problem Cause Solution Prevention
    Curling Humidity changes, one-sided coating Weight down, humidify/dehumidify room Store properly, use heavier weight
    Tearing When Folding Paper too thick, folding against grain Score before folding, fold with grain Test grain direction first, use bone folder
    Ink Bleeding Paper too absorbent, wrong ink Use thicker paper, different ink, seal paper Test on scrap, use smooth cardstock
    Yellowing Acidic paper, UV exposure Use acid-free paper, UV-protective storage Buy archival quality, store properly
    Won’t Feed Through Printer Too thick, wrong finish Check printer specs, hand-feed, adjust settings Know printer limits (usually 110 lb max)
    Dull Colors After Printing Wrong paper finish, ink type Use glossy for photos, matte for graphics Match paper to ink and project

    Scoring and Folding Guidelines

    Paper Weight Scoring Required? Scoring Tool Folding Technique
    20-60 lb No None, or bone folder for crisp edge Fold and crease with bone folder
    65-80 lb Recommended Bone folder, scoring board Score first, fold along score line
    80-110 lb Yes Scoring board, embossing tool Deep score, fold carefully, burnish
    110 lb+ Absolutely Scoring tool, ruler, pressure Multiple score passes, fold slowly, press firmly

    Scoring Direction: Always score on the inside of the fold (where the fold compresses) for clean results.

    Adhesives by Paper Type

    Paper Type Best Adhesive Avoid Application Tips
    Thin (20-60 lb) Glue stick, spray adhesive, double-sided tape Liquid glue (warps) Apply sparingly, work quickly
    Cardstock (65-110 lb) Liquid glue, tape runner, foam adhesive Hot glue (too bulky) Use enough for hold, not excess
    Vellum/Translucent Vellum adhesive, mini dots, corners only Standard glue (shows through) Minimal, hide under embellishments
    Metallic/Glossy Tape runner, strong double-sided tape Liquid glue (slides, doesn’t stick) Clean surface first, firm pressure
    Textured Liquid glue, strong tape Weak adhesives (won’t grip texture) Press into texture, extra drying time
    Chipboard/Heavy Hot glue, strong liquid glue, brads Glue stick (too weak) Clamp or weight while drying

    Testing New Paper

    Test How to Perform What It Reveals Before Starting
    Grain Direction Gently bend both directions without creasing Folding direction, tear direction Large projects, folded cards
    Ink Compatibility Print/stamp on scrap Bleeding, drying time, color vibrancy Printing, stamping projects
    Fold Test Score and fold a scrap piece Cracking, clean fold, scoring needs Cards, boxes, dimensional projects
    Adhesive Test Apply various adhesives to scrap Which adhesive works best, show-through Multi-material projects
    Color Fastness Expose sample to light for days Fading susceptibility Archival, displayed projects
    Eraser Test Erase pencil marks Surface durability, texture retention Projects with pencil marks, drawing

    Common Paper Terms Glossary

    Term Definition Why It Matters
    GSM Grams per square meter—universal weight measure Reliable comparison across brands/countries
    Acid-Free pH neutral, won’t yellow or deteriorate rapidly Critical for archival projects, photos
    Lignin-Free Wood component removed (causes yellowing) Archival quality, long-term preservation
    Buffered Alkaline reserve to neutralize acids Extra protection for archival storage
    Cotton Content Percentage of cotton fibers vs. wood pulp Durability, luxury feel, longevity
    Tooth Surface texture, affects media adherence Important for drawing, painting
    Deckle Edge Rough, feathered edge (handmade look) Decorative, artisan appearance
    Cold Press Textured watercolor paper surface Good for most watercolor techniques
    Hot Press Smooth watercolor paper surface Detail work, ink, smooth washes

    Conclusion and Key Takeaways

    Paper selection impacts every aspect of your craft: durability, appearance, functionality, and longevity. Start with understanding weight (GSM is most reliable), choose acid-free for anything archival, match finish to your project, and always test before committing to large quantities.

    For most crafters, a starter collection should include:

    • 80 lb cardstock in white and basic colors
    • 65 lb patterned scrapbook paper
    • Copy paper for templates and origami
    • Vellum for overlays
    • One specialty paper that excites you

    Build your collection based on the crafts you love, buy quality for final projects and budget options for practice, and always store paper properly to maintain its quality. Paper is the foundation of paper crafts—invest in understanding it, and your projects will reflect that knowledge.
